Bottom line

CVS's greatest strength is its biggest liability — aggressive vertical integration. The refocus: govern the core, divest Omnicare, out-tech the disruptors.

Overview

CVS Health's aggressive vertical integration — pharmacy, insurance (Aetna), PBM (Caremark), and provider services (Oak Street, HealthHUB) — built a healthcare ecosystem no rival can match, and simultaneously became its biggest source of legal, financial, and reputational exposure. This strategy project diagnosed that tension and delivered a concrete refocus plan, framed for a board-level audience.

Analysis
  • Industry structure — a concentrated oligopoly where 80%+ of revenue comes from prescriptions; Porter's Five Forces shows high buyer power (PBM leverage), high supplier power, and high substitution risk.
  • The core tension — the same integration that creates advantage (Aetna + Caremark + Oak Street) draws antitrust scrutiny and erodes public trust; the Omnicare unit became the cautionary tale, filing Chapter 11 after a federal judgment.
  • Digital substitution — online prescription ordering is growing ~10% annually, with 53% of Americans ordering online in 2023; Amazon Pharmacy and telehealth bypass the traditional model.
  • Competitive landscape — UnitedHealth (Optum) and Kaiser Permanente run the same integrated playbook, raising the bar for execution.

Recommendation

A three-part refocus: deepen and govern the core integration (Aetna–Caremark–Oak Street) behind legal and ethical firewalls that demonstrate pro-competitive value; divest Omnicare — low coordination with the core, outsized legal risk, and sale proceeds that strengthen the balance sheet; and aggressively implement technology across the ecosystem to compete with Amazon and the telehealth wave rather than be displaced by it.
