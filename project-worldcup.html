Bottom line

67.3% of World Cup matches called correctly — trained only on pre-tournament data, verified on 98 real games.

Overview

Probabilistic forecasts for the 2026 FIFA World Cup — built mid-tournament, during semifinals week, and scored live against the matches already played. The engine adapts my AFCON 2025 predictor (63.5% accuracy on that tournament) to the World Cup, pairing an opponent-adjusted Poisson model with a squad-market-value prior and simulating the remaining bracket through to championship odds.

Both models were trained only on data available before the tournament, so every reported number is honestly out-of-sample: 67.3% accuracy across the 98 matches played through the quarterfinals.

How it works
  • Opponent-adjusted ratings — attack and defence ratings fit jointly over every team (Dixon-Coles-style alternating updates), with time-decay weighting and neutral-venue handling.
  • Squad-value prior — ratings shrink toward Transfermarkt squad market values instead of a global average. The prior's weight was tuned on AFCON 2025 — a different tournament — so the World Cup evaluation stays untouched. This was the single biggest accuracy gain: +9 points over the results-only model.
  • Classifier benchmark — a multinomial logistic model over learned team embeddings and point-in-time form features, built with a temporal validation split and no look-ahead leakage.
  • Honest evaluation — scored on real tournament matches using accuracy, log loss, Brier score, and ranked probability score, against frequency and uniform baselines.
  • Live bracket simulation — Monte Carlo over the remaining fixtures; refreshing the daily results dataset automatically narrows the simulation to what is still unplayed.
Results & takeaways

The value-prior Poisson model reached 67.3% accuracy — 73% in the knockout rounds — beating the results-only Poisson (58.2%), the classifier (52.0%), and both baselines on every metric. Championship odds for the remaining bracket update live in the demo above.

The project shows model validation done right: out-of-sample testing, temporal splits, calibrated probability metrics rather than accuracy alone, and hyperparameters tuned on a separate tournament to avoid leakage.
